# Product Map Ops

Most affiliate sites cover the 3-5 "hero" products in a niche (the thing people search for by name) and miss the 20-50 smaller items that actual practitioners buy over time. The hero products get the traffic; the long tail often gets the *margin and repeat purchases*. This skill's job is completeness, not judgment — score the output with `product-scout-ops` afterward.

## The enumeration method

Work through every layer below for the niche. Don't skip layers because they seem obvious — the point is systematic coverage, not intuition.

**1. Core/hero products**
The thing someone searches for by name. (Drip irrigation → drip irrigation kit, drip irrigation timer.)

**2. Consumables and wear items**
Anything the core product uses up or that degrades and needs replacing. (Drippers, emitters, filters, batteries for timers, tubing that UV-degrades.) This layer is usually where recurring revenue lives — flag every item here explicitly as "repeat purchase" for product-scout-ops.

**3. Connective/fitting hardware**
The unglamorous stuff that holds the system together — the kind of item nobody searches for by itself but every practitioner ends up buying repeatedly because they lose or break them. (Barbed connectors, elbow fittings, tee fittings, goof plugs, hole punch tools, tubing stakes, end caps.)

**4. Tools required to install/maintain**
Anything needed to set up or service the core product that isn't part of the kit. (Punch tools, tubing cutters, pressure regulators, backflow preventers.)

**5. Adjacent/complementary categories**
Things a buyer in this niche also needs, even if not directly part of the core system. (For drip irrigation: pots/containers suited to drip setups, moisture meters, mulch, plant food delivered via drip.)

**6. Upgrade/expansion path**
What does someone buy after year one, once satisfied with the basic setup? (Smart/WiFi timer upgrade, zone expansion kits, larger reservoir.)

**7. Failure-mode replacement items**
What breaks or fails that isn't a "consumable" per se but gets replaced reactively? (Cracked fittings after freeze, chewed tubing, clogged emitters needing full replacement vs. cleaning.)

## Sourcing techniques (use several, not just one)

- **Competitor teardown**: find the top 3-5 existing sites/YouTube channels in the niche and list every distinct product they link to or mention, not just their headline recommendations.
- **"Complete setup" reconstruction**: search for "[niche] complete setup guide" or "[niche] everything you need" content and extract every named item.
- **Retailer "frequently bought together" / "customers also bought" mining**: on Amazon or the niche's dedicated retailer (e.g., Drip Depot, RainPoint), look at what's bundled or cross-sold with the hero products.
- **Forum/Reddit mining**: search "[niche] subreddit what did I forget" or "[niche] beginner mistakes" — beginners posting about what they didn't know they needed is a goldmine for the connective-hardware and tools layers.
- **Manual/instruction teardown**: if a core product has an installation manual or instructional video, every tool or part it mentions using is a candidate.

## Output format

Produce a single table, grouped by layer, for the niche:

| Layer | Product/Item | Est. Price Range | Repeat Purchase? | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|

Aim for genuine exhaustiveness — for an established niche like drip irrigation, a real list should land in the 25-50 item range across all layers, not 8-10. If your list is short, you likely stopped after layers 1-2 and skipped the connective hardware, tools, and failure-mode layers where most of the missed revenue lives.

## Handoff

Once the list exists, hand it to `product-scout-ops` to score each item/category on commission economics, then cross-reference against the site's existing content to find:
- **Coverage gaps**: items with no existing article/product page at all
- **Consumable priority**: repeat-purchase items that should get dedicated "which one to buy" content, since they drive recurring affiliate clicks from the same returning readers

## Important caveats

- Don't fabricate specific product names, prices, or brand claims you haven't verified — web search real products/prices for a given niche rather than inventing plausible-sounding ones, especially before the user acts on price or brand info.
- This skill produces breadth, not a buying recommendation — resist the urge to pre-filter "obviously low value" items out of the list. Let product-scout-ops make that call with real scoring, since low-cost connective items sometimes turn out to have surprisingly good commission economics.

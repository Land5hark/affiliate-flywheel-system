# Content Differentiation Checklist — Google Spam Policy Risk Mitigation

## Why this exists

A portfolio of many small, templated, agent-generated affiliate sites is exactly the pattern Google's Helpful Content system and site reputation abuse policy were built to detect. This isn't a theoretical risk — it's the single biggest threat to a "hundreds of microblogs" flywheel, because a network-level deindexing event can take out many sites at once rather than degrading gracefully one at a time. Design against it from blog #1, not after blog #40 gets hit.

## What actually gets flagged

- Many sites with visually/structurally identical templates and near-identical article scaffolding, differing only in swapped niche terms
- Content with no genuine specificity — generic "best X for Y in 2026" filler that could apply to any product in any category with find-and-replace
- Thin sites that exist primarily to host affiliate links with minimal original value-add
- Sites that appear to be part of a coordinated network (shared hosting patterns, shared analytics IDs, cross-linking between portfolio sites, similar registration dates/patterns)

## Mitigations to build into the trio's instructions (not optional, not "nice to have")

1. **Genuine structural variation between blogs.** Don't reuse the exact same Hugo theme/template unmodified across every site. Vary layout, vary the "About" framing, vary how comparison tables are presented. This can be templated *generation* logic (different template variants selected per blog) without requiring hand-design per site.

2. **Real specificity requirement per article** (enforced by the Editor, see agent-trio-roles.md). Every article needs at least one piece of content that couldn't have been generated by swapping niche terms into a generic template — a real comparison point, a real tradeoff, a real edge case specific to that product category.

3. **No cross-linking between portfolio blogs** unless there's a genuine topical reason a reader would benefit — don't build an internal link network between your own sites as an SEO shortcut. That's exactly the "coordinated network" signal.

4. **Vary technical fingerprints where reasonable** — separate analytics properties per site at minimum. (Full infrastructure separation, like separate hosting accounts, is a heavier lift — decide how far to take this based on how much risk tolerance you have as the portfolio grows; flag it as a decision point rather than assuming either extreme.)

5. **Genuinely useful non-affiliate content.** Sites shouldn't read as purely a wrapper around affiliate links — real troubleshooting content, real buyer questions answered honestly (including "you probably don't need X" when that's true), builds the kind of trust signal that both readers and Google's systems reward.

6. **Monitor for policy updates.** Google's spam policies evolve. Whoever (you or an agent) periodically checks Google's Search Central documentation for changes to the Helpful Content or site reputation abuse policies, since a flywheel operating on stale assumptions about what's safe is a real risk at this scale.

## What this checklist is not

This isn't a guarantee against deindexing — no checklist is. It's the difference between "obviously and mechanically gaming search" and "a genuinely useful small site that happens to be efficiently produced." The goal is the latter, consistently, across every blog the flywheel spins up — not cutting corners once you're past the first few and it feels routine.
